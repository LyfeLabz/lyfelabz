# LYFELABZ HQIM LESSON REVISION PROCESS

The standard operating procedure for revising an existing lesson into a flagship
HQIM lesson.

This is the instructional equivalent of the Lesson Factory. The Lesson Factory
governs how a new lesson is built. This document governs how an existing lesson
is raised to the flagship standard without losing what already makes it good. It
is written for the curriculum, not the code. It names instructional work, not
HTML.

It sits on top of three documents and does not repeat them:

* `HQIM_LESSON_FRAMEWORK.md` defines the seventeen instructional elements a
  flagship lesson must contain and the 🧠 Show Your Thinking step that closes it.
* `LYFELABZ_INSTRUCTIONAL_ARCHETYPES.md` defines the five kinds of thinking a
  LyfeLabz lesson can be built to carry.
* `TIER_C_HQIM_CONVERSION_BLUEPRINT.md` is the worked example of this process on
  the oldest lessons.

Read those first. This document tells you the order in which to use them.

---

## Why a process, and not just a checklist

The framework tells you what a flagship lesson contains. It does not tell you how
to get a specific, already-written lesson there without flattening its
personality. That is the hard part, and it is the part a checklist cannot do on
its own.

The failure mode this process exists to prevent is templating: dragging every
lesson toward the shape of Earth's Layers until the curriculum becomes fifty
copies of one causal chain. Earth's Layers is the gold standard for a mechanism
lesson. It is the wrong shape for What Is Life, which is a classification lesson,
and the wrong shape for Layers of Time, which is an evidence lesson. A revision
that makes What Is Life look like Earth's Layers has made it worse, even if every
box is now checked.

So the goal is stated plainly and holds over every phase below:

**Preserve each lesson's unique strengths while ensuring every lesson meets the
HQIM instructional standard.**

Not identical. Equally strong, each in its own kind.

---

## The Revision Workflow

Eight phases. The first six are thinking. The seventh is the only phase that
touches the lesson. The eighth confirms the work. Do not begin editing until
Phases 1 through 6 are done, because the whole point is that the edits in Phase 7
are deliberate consequences of the analysis, not improvisation.

A note before starting: read the entire lesson end to end before Phase 1, the way
a student would meet it. You cannot judge strengths, gaps, or thinking from
section headings.

---

### Phase 1. Identify the instructional archetype

Before anything else, decide what kind of thinking this lesson is really for. The
archetype governs every later phase: which strengths matter, which gaps are
critical, what an authentic phenomenon looks like, and which Show Your Thinking
format fits.

Work from the driving question first, because the driving question is the most
honest statement of instructional intent. Then confirm against the lesson's
interactives and the misconceptions it has to confront, exactly as
`LYFELABZ_INSTRUCTIONAL_ARCHETYPES.md` did. Assign one primary archetype from the
five:

1. Phenomenon and Mechanism: why does this happen?
2. Systems and Cycles: how do the parts interact?
3. Evidence and Inference: how do we know?
4. Classification and Identity: what is it, and how can we tell?
5. Design and Decisions: what should we build or choose, and at what cost?

Name a secondary archetype only if the lesson genuinely spans two, as biological
evolution spans Mechanism and Evidence. Most lessons have one. Do not invent a
secondary to seem thorough.

Write down why. One short paragraph: what the driving question promises, and
which signals confirm it. This paragraph is the spine of the whole revision. If
the archetype is wrong here, every later phase inherits the error.

The Coverage Map in the archetypes document already assigns most lessons. Use it
as a starting point, but verify against the lesson in front of you. A lesson may
have drifted from its stated archetype over past edits.

---

### Phase 2. Identify and protect the lesson's strengths

Before looking for what is missing, find what is working and mark it untouchable.
This phase comes second on purpose. It is easy to arrive at a lesson with the
framework in hand and see only gaps. The strengths are what make this lesson
memorable, and they are the first casualty of a careless revision.

List the specific instructional moves that must not change. Be concrete. Not
"the Engage is good" but "the alive / not alive / debated sort front-loads
non-examples and a genuinely contested case, which is stronger misconception work
than the flagship." Preservation targets usually include:

* The driving question and its exact wording.
* The anchoring phenomenon or sorting challenge, especially non-examples and
  debated cases.
* Any interactive that lets the student manipulate the real idea.
* A wrap-up, analogy, or diagram that carries the lesson's voice.
* The click-to-reveal vocabulary, dual-coded diagrams, and the two-mode quiz.

For each, say in a sentence what instructional job it does, so that in Phase 6
you can tell the difference between a change that strengthens it and a change that
would quietly kill it. A strength you cannot articulate is a strength you will
edit away by accident.

The blueprint's finding on What Is Life is the lesson of this phase: several Tier
C lessons already exceed the flagship on misconception work and phenomenon
design. Those are models to copy outward, not gaps to fill.

---

### Phase 3. Compare against the HQIM Lesson Framework

Now, and only now, go looking for what is missing. Walk the lesson against the
seventeen elements in `HQIM_LESSON_FRAMEWORK.md` and the Flagship Checklist,
element by element, in Standard Lesson Order.

For each element, record one of: present and strong, present but shallow, or
missing. The blueprint's warning governs this phase: do not mistake "already has
the section" for "already HQIM." What Is Life had every section and still lacked
the one thing that mattered most, a place where the student constructs an
explanation. Audit for instructional depth, not section presence. A Quick Recall
that exists but asks one thin question is "present but shallow," not "present."

Then prioritize the gaps by educational impact, not by how easy they are to fix.
Use four levels:

* **Critical.** The lesson cannot be called flagship without this. In practice
  the recurring critical gap across the program is the student-constructed
  explanation, per the framework's top priority. Treat its absence as critical in
  every lesson until proven otherwise.
* **High.** A gap that meaningfully weakens learning, such as a wrap-up that
  returns to the question but never synthesizes the arc, or a missing prediction
  gate in a misconception-dense lesson.
* **Medium.** A gap that improves encoding or retrieval but is not load-bearing,
  such as inline-bold terms that should be Key Idea cards, or a quiz score board
  missing its mystery loop.
* **Low.** Verification and polish: aria labels, honest badging, Connections
  wording, keeping Educator notes in sync.

The priority is what drives the revision plan in Phase 6. Rank by what changes
the student most.

---

### Phase 4. Evaluate the lesson's scientific thinking

Sections can all be present and the lesson can still fail to make a student
think. This phase steps back from the checklist and judges the quality of the
reasoning the lesson demands. Answer each question honestly, in writing:

* **What kind of thinking are students actually doing?** Match the observed
  thinking to the Phase 1 archetype. If the archetype says "infer the unseen from
  evidence" but every task is recognition, the lesson is not yet teaching its own
  kind of thinking. A telling test: if clicking is the only verb the student
  uses, the lesson has not reached the standard.
* **Is the phenomenon authentic?** Is the anchoring event something a curious
  student could have wondered about, and does the science genuinely explain it?
  Or is it a decorative hook the lesson never returns to?
* **Is there productive struggle?** Is there at least one moment where the
  student does cognitive work the page cannot do for them, scaffolded and always
  resolved? Or is every difficulty removed before it can do any teaching?
* **Are misconceptions surfaced and resolved?** Where a well-known wrong idea
  exists, is it named and confronted so the correct model displaces it, rather
  than sitting quietly beside it?
* **Does the lesson build a coherent explanation?** Do the sections add up to one
  connected argument that answers the driving question, or a sequence of separate
  facts?
* **Does the lesson support long-term retention?** Are there retrieval events,
  spacing, and a synthesis, so the learning is built to last past the period it
  was taught in?

This phase often surfaces the real work of the revision, which is frequently not
a missing section but shallow thinking inside a section that is technically
present.

---

### Phase 5. Choose the Show Your Thinking format

🧠 Show Your Thinking is the required close of every flagship lesson and, in most
lessons, the single most important thing this whole process adds, because it is
usually the only place the student constructs rather than selects. Choosing its
format well is choosing the lesson's culminating act of thinking, so it gets its
own phase.

Pick exactly one of the three approved formats, and let the Phase 1 archetype
lead the choice:

* **Explain in One Sentence.** A single, well-formed statement of the central
  idea. Strong for Classification and Identity, where the student states the
  deciding criterion and applies it, and for any lesson whose mechanism reduces
  to one clean sentence.
* **Build the Explanation.** The student arranges statements or cause-and-effect
  relationships into the correct explanation. This is the preferred default
  whenever it fits, because it measures reasoning without demanding extended
  writing. It is the natural fit for Phenomenon and Mechanism (a causal chain to
  order) and for Systems and Cycles (a loop or cascade to trace).
* **Mini CER.** A very brief claim, evidence, reasoning response. The signature
  fit for Evidence and Inference, which is literally about claim and evidence, and
  for Design and Decisions, where a defended choice is a claim about tradeoffs.

Write down why the chosen format fits this lesson's thinking. If two formats seem
possible, prefer the one that forces the student to construct more of the logic
themselves. The format must synthesize the lesson's central idea, never ask for
an isolated definition, and sit immediately before Submit.

---

### Phase 6. Design the revision plan

Turn the analysis into a plan. Still no HTML. Think only about learning.

The plan is an ordered list of instructional changes, each tied to a gap from
Phase 3 or a weakness from Phase 4, and each respecting a strength from Phase 2.
For every proposed change, state three things:

1. The gap or weakness it addresses, and its priority.
2. What the student will now do that they could not do before.
3. The strength it must not disturb, and how it stays intact.

Sequence the plan by priority, critical first. In practice the recommended order,
adapted from the blueprint, is: confirm the preserved core is intact, add or
deepen the Key Idea reveals, strengthen the wrap-up into a real synthesis, add
the one open-response sensemaking prompt, choose and place Show Your Thinking, add
the quiz mystery loop, then backfill Educator notes for everything new.

Match the shape of every addition to the lesson's archetype. A classification
lesson gets a criteria synthesis, not a forced cause-effect chain. A systems
lesson gets a loop, not a straight line. This is where the discipline of Phase 1
pays off: the plan should read as changes that belong to this lesson, not changes
imported from Earth's Layers.

If the plan starts to look like a redesign, stop. Preservation mode is in force.
The plan should be additive and surgical. If a lesson seems to need a redesign,
that is a decision for the curriculum owner, not a revision to perform quietly.

---

### Phase 7. Implement the revision

Now, and only now, edit the lesson. Follow the Phase 6 plan in its stated order.

Preserve every strength named in Phase 2. Add only what the plan calls for. Do
not opportunistically clean up, re-theme, or refactor neighboring code. Match the
lesson's own voice, spacing, color, and card style; imitate neighboring lessons,
not a different lesson's design.

Reuse the existing architecture. Use the established vocabulary card, quiz, and
classroom-mode patterns rather than inventing new ones. New copy follows the
house style: no em dashes, use a spaced hyphen; student-facing language in every
student-visible surface; `KEY IDEA: [TERM]` for explanatory cards and `NAME IT`
only for literal identification. Add Educator Mode notes for every new element as
you add it, rated conservatively, so the rationale never falls out of sync.

Keep the diff small and legible. A reviewer should be able to see that each change
maps to a line in the plan.

---

### Phase 8. Self-audit

Confirm the lesson has improved without losing its identity. Evaluate the revised
lesson against all five standards:

* **HQIM Lesson Framework.** Every mandatory element present, accurate, and in
  Standard Lesson Order. Walk the Flagship Checklist.
* **Instructional Archetype.** The additions match the kind of thinking named in
  Phase 1. Nothing was imported from a different archetype's shape.
* **Show Your Thinking.** The chosen format is present, immediately before Submit,
  synthesizes the central idea, and asks the student to construct rather than
  select.
* **MA 2016 Science Standards.** The science is accurate. Badging is honest:
  badge only standards actually taught and assessed; supporting concepts may be
  taught without claiming their badge.
* **Existing LyfeLabz instructional philosophy.** Preservation intact, voice
  intact, student-facing language throughout, Connections reveal relationships
  rather than progression, no em dashes.

Then apply the quality-control pass from the project instructions: verify in the
browser, check the console for errors, test the interactions, confirm responsive
behavior, and grep for em dashes. Report exactly what changed. Do not claim
verification that was not performed.

Finish by answering the two questions that define success for this whole process:

1. Is every Phase 2 strength still intact?
2. Would a curious middle school student now do more real thinking than before?

If either answer is no, the revision is not done.

## Guiding Principles

These hold over every phase and override novelty.

* **Preserve before replacing.** The first question is what already works, not
  what is missing. Strengths are the hardest thing to rebuild and the easiest to
  lose.
* **Improve instructional quality, not novelty.** The best lesson is not the one
  with the most features. It is the one students can successfully learn from.
* **Different archetypes require different instructional moves.** A mechanism, a
  system, an inference, a classification, and a design decision each ask for
  different thinking, different phenomena, and different Show Your Thinking.
* **Do not force every lesson into the Earth's Layers structure.** Earth's Layers
  is the gold standard for a causal chain. Match the wrap-up and Key Idea shape to
  the lesson's own logic instead of copying the flagship literally.
* **Maintain lesson personality.** Voice, pacing, and the signature moves that
  make a lesson memorable are part of its quality, not decoration to standardize
  away.
* **Engagement should emerge from authentic scientific or engineering thinking.**
  Not from added features, animation, or novelty. If the thinking is real, the
  engagement follows.
* **Every revision should strengthen coherence, curiosity, scientific reasoning,
  and long-term retention.** If a proposed change serves none of these four, cut
  it from the plan.

## HQIM Lesson Revision Checklist

The pre-flight review before revising any lesson. Short by design.

**Before editing (Phases 1 to 6, all thinking):**

- [ ] Read the whole lesson end to end as a student would.
- [ ] Primary archetype assigned, with a one-paragraph reason. Secondary only if
  the lesson genuinely spans two.
- [ ] Strengths listed and marked untouchable, each with the job it does.
- [ ] Every framework element rated present-and-strong, present-but-shallow, or
  missing, then prioritized Critical / High / Medium / Low.
- [ ] Scientific thinking judged: authentic phenomenon, productive struggle,
  misconceptions resolved, coherent explanation, retention support.
- [ ] Confirmed the student constructs an explanation somewhere, not only selects.
- [ ] Show Your Thinking format chosen to fit the archetype, with a reason.
- [ ] Revision plan written, ordered by priority, each change tied to a gap and to
  a preserved strength, shaped to this lesson's logic.

**While editing (Phase 7):**

- [ ] Only planned changes made; no redesign, re-theme, or opportunistic cleanup.
- [ ] Every Phase 2 strength preserved.
- [ ] House style held: no em dashes, student-facing language, `KEY IDEA:` versus
  `NAME IT`, existing quiz and vocabulary architecture reused.
- [ ] Educator Mode notes added for every new element, rated conservatively.

**After editing (Phase 8):**

- [ ] Audited against the Framework, the Archetype, Show Your Thinking, MA 2016
  standards, and LyfeLabz philosophy.
- [ ] Verified in browser, console clean, interactions and responsive behavior
  tested, em dashes grepped.
- [ ] Exactly what changed reported, with no unperformed verification claimed.
- [ ] Both success questions answered yes: strengths intact, and the student now
  does more real thinking than before.
