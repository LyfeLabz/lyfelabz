# LyfeLabz Instructional Archetypes

An instructional analysis of the LyfeLabz curriculum, written from the seat of a
curriculum director. The question is not "how are these lessons built" but "what
fundamental kinds of science learning experiences do they contain."

This document does not recommend revisions or implementation changes. It names
the archetypes that emerge from the curriculum as it already exists.

---

## Method

The analysis began with no assumption that archetypes exist. Fifty lessons were
read for the one thing that reveals instructional intent most honestly: the
driving question. A driving question is a promise about the kind of thinking a
student will do. "How does Earth's internal structure cause changes on the
surface" promises causal reasoning. "If a virus can replicate and evolve, what's
missing that keeps it from being truly alive" promises reasoning about criteria
and category. Two lessons can share a grade, a unit, and a visual template and
still ask for completely different cognitive work.

The driving questions were then cross-checked against three other signals:

* The interactives each lesson leans on (an orbit model, a sortable timeline, a
  classification sorter, a manipulable system with feedback).
* The standing interactive library (simulations, games, investigations,
  extensions), since a resource type reveals what kind of thinking its parent
  lessons reward.
* The misconceptions each lesson has to confront.

When these signals agreed, a cluster was real. When a single lesson stood alone,
it was tested against the nearest cluster before any new archetype was proposed.

The conclusion: the curriculum genuinely clusters, and it needs **five**
archetypes to describe all of it. Not more. A sixth candidate (a separate
astronomy archetype) was considered and rejected for reasons given at the end.

---

## The Five Archetypes at a Glance

| Archetype | The core question | The thinking |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Phenomenon and Mechanism | Why does this happen? | Build a cause-and-effect model of one phenomenon |
| 2. Systems and Cycles | How do the parts interact? | Trace flows, feedback, and cascading change across many parts |
| 3. Evidence and Inference | How do we know? | Reconstruct the unseen or the past from clues |
| 4. Classification and Identity | What is it, and how can we tell? | Apply criteria to decide category or identity |
| 5. Design and Decisions | What should we build or choose, and at what cost? | Reason about constraints, tradeoffs, and human impact |

Everything else about a LyfeLabz lesson - the hero, the vocabulary cards, the
quiz flow, Show Your Thinking - is shared scaffolding defined by the HQIM
framework. The archetypes are not competing structures. They are five different
kinds of thinking that the one shared structure is asked to carry.

---

## Archetype 1: Phenomenon and Mechanism

**Central thinking.** The student builds a cause-and-effect model of a single
natural phenomenon and can run that model forward to a new case. The payoff
question is always some form of "why does this happen" or "how does this cause
that."

**Instructional purpose.** To move a student from knowing that something happens
to understanding the mechanism by which it happens, so they can predict what
would change if a cause changed. This is the spine of the whole curriculum and
the archetype the gold standard was written for.

**Typical lesson flow.** A concrete, puzzling event anchors the lesson. The
student predicts an outcome before the mechanism is revealed. The mechanism is
then delivered as an explicit causal chain (stored energy releases, so the
ground shakes; warmer particles collide with cooler ones, so heat moves). A
misconception checkpoint names the intuitive-but-wrong version. The wrap-up
returns to the opening event and answers the driving question with the model the
student just built.

**Phenomena that work best.** Single, observable, causally clean events. A
carpet shock, a plucked string, a shaking floor, a plant that gains mass from
apparently nothing, a Moon that does not fall. The best phenomena are ones a
student can picture and has probably wondered about, where one dominant cause
does most of the explaining.

**Interactives that support it best.** Step-through causal animations and
manipulable single-variable models: a slider that changes amplitude and shows
the effect, an orbit model where gravity and motion trade off, a photon moving
through a medium. The interactive should let the student vary the cause and see
the effect, which is the mechanism made touchable.

**Common misconceptions.** These lessons are misconception-dense by nature.
Heat is a substance that flows. Waves carry matter along with them. Plants get
their mass from soil. The Moon's phase is Earth's shadow. Gravity switches off in
space. The mechanism has to displace the intuition, not sit beside it.

**Appropriate Show Your Thinking.** Build the Explanation is the natural fit,
because a mechanism is a chain the student can assemble in the right causal
order. Explain in One Sentence works when the mechanism reduces to a single
clean statement.

**Flagship lessons.** `lesson_earths-layers.html` is the program's gold standard
and the purest example. Also `lesson_nature-of-waves.html`,
`lesson_heat-transfer.html`, `lesson_energy-transfer.html`,
`lesson_earthquakes.html`, `lesson_photosynthesis.html`, and the astronomy
lessons (`lesson_eclipses.html`, `lesson_phases-of-the-moon.html`,
`lesson_gravity.html`, `lesson_sun-earth-moon.html`), which are the
misconception-intensive edge of this archetype rather than an archetype of their
own.

## Archetype 2: Systems and Cycles

**Central thinking.** The student reasons about many parts at once: how they
interact, how matter or energy flows among them, how feedback keeps a system
stable, and how a change or failure in one part ripples through the whole. The
payoff question is "how do the parts depend on one another" or "why does changing
one part affect everything."

**Instructional purpose.** To grow the systems reasoning that single-mechanism
lessons cannot, because a system behaves in ways no single part explains.
Cascading failure, feedback, and cycling are emergent, and a student has to hold
the whole in mind to see them.

**Typical lesson flow.** The lesson introduces the parts, then the interactions,
then perturbs the system so the student can watch a change propagate. The
sensemaking moment is tracing a ripple: remove a predator, cut a supply line,
overload one organ system, and follow the consequences around the whole.

**Phenomena that work best.** Anything with interdependent parts and loops:
body systems working together during a sprint, a food chain losing energy at each
step, carbon or water cycling endlessly, an engineered system where one failure
stops everything, an ecosystem knocked off balance.

**Interactives that support it best.** Manipulable whole-system models with
feedback, where the student changes one input and the system responds: a
population model, a nutrient or energy cycle the student can trace, a system
where disabling one node cascades. Simulations serve this archetype especially
well because a system has to be run, not just read.

**Common misconceptions.** Linear, one-cause thinking (a change has only one
effect). Believing matter or energy is used up rather than transformed or
cycled. Ignoring feedback. Assuming a system returns to normal on its own after
any disturbance.

**Appropriate Show Your Thinking.** Build the Explanation, arranged as a chain or
loop of interactions rather than a single line, because the student is showing
how a change moves through the system. Mini CER fits stability and impact
lessons where the student argues what will happen to a system and why.

**Flagship lessons.** `lesson_ecosystem-stability.html` and
`lesson_engineering-systems.html` are the clearest. Also
`lesson_body-systems.html`, `lesson_energy-flow.html`,
`lesson_carbon-cycle.html`, `lesson_water-cycle.html`,
`lesson_parts-of-an-ecosystem.html`, `lesson_human-impacts.html`,
`lesson_transportation-systems.html`, `lesson_structural-systems.html`,
`lesson_communication-systems.html`, and
`lesson_renewable-and-nonrenewable-resources.html`.

**Why this is separate from Mechanism.** It would be tempting to fold Systems
into Phenomenon and Mechanism, since a system is causal too. They are kept apart
because the thinking, the interactive, and the misconception all differ. A
mechanism lesson isolates one dominant cause; a system lesson is about the
interactions no single cause explains. A mechanism wants a single-variable model;
a system wants a feedback model that can be perturbed. A mechanism confronts a
wrong cause; a system confronts linear thinking itself. A curriculum director
designing the two would reach for different phenomena and different interactives,
which is the test for a real archetype.

## Archetype 3: Evidence and Inference

**Central thinking.** The student reasons from clues to a conclusion about
something they cannot directly see: the deep past, a vast scale, an unobservable
process. The payoff question is "how do we know," not "how does it work."

**Instructional purpose.** To teach that science is a way of knowing, not just a
body of known things. These lessons make the reasoning visible: the student sees
how scattered evidence forces a conclusion, and learns to trust or doubt a claim
based on its evidence.

**Typical lesson flow.** The lesson presents a claim about the unseen (the
continents were once joined; these layers record millions of years; that light
left a star long ago) and then walks the student through the evidence that
supports it, ideally letting the student do the inferring before the conclusion
is confirmed. The struggle is holding uncertainty and weighing clues.

**Phenomena that work best.** Anything where the truth cannot be observed
directly and must be inferred: rock and fossil layers, matching coastlines and
fossils across oceans, whale ancestry written in bones, the night sky as a record
of distance and time. The meta-lesson on how trustworthy evidence is produced
belongs here too.

**Interactives that support it best.** Sortable and orderable evidence:
timelines the student sequences, layers the student reads, fossils and clues the
student arranges into a story. Investigations and evidence hunts fit naturally,
because the student's job is to assemble a case rather than run a model.

**Common misconceptions.** That the distant past is unknowable or a guess. That
older means lower or simpler in a rigid way. Confusing correlation with cause in
an evidence set. Treating a scientific inference as mere opinion because no one
watched it happen.

**Appropriate Show Your Thinking.** Mini CER is the signature format here,
because these lessons are literally about claim, evidence, and reasoning. Build
the Explanation works when the evidence has to be put in order to tell the story.

**Flagship lessons.** `lesson_layers-of-time.html` and
`lesson_continental-drift.html` are the clearest. `lesson_conducting-experiments.html`
belongs here as the meta-lesson on how evidence itself is made trustworthy.
`lesson_biological-evolution.html` and `lesson_earths-place-in-the-universe.html`
sit on the border with Mechanism and Systems and draw heavily on this archetype
for their "how do we know" spine.

## Archetype 4: Classification and Identity

**Central thinking.** The student applies a set of criteria to decide what
something is or which category it belongs to. The payoff question is "what counts
as this" or "how can we tell." The interesting cases are the borderline ones,
where the criteria are tested.

**Instructional purpose.** To teach that scientific categories are defined by
properties and criteria, not by appearance or intuition, and that the hard cases
are where the definition earns its keep. A virus, a dissolved-then-recovered
solute, a floating steel ship: each forces the student to apply a rule rather
than a hunch.

**Typical lesson flow.** The lesson establishes the criteria, then applies them
to clear cases, then hands the student a hard case that sits on the boundary. The
sensemaking moment is a judgment call defended by the criteria, not by first
impression.

**Phenomena that work best.** Ambiguous or counterintuitive membership
questions. Is a virus alive. Is a new substance formed. Is this a pure substance
or a mixture. Will it float or sink, and what is it made of. The best phenomena
are the ones where intuition and criteria disagree.

**Interactives that support it best.** Sorters and classifiers: drag-to-category
tools, is-it-or-isn't-it games, property tables the student uses to identify an
unknown, gray-zone challenges built specifically around borderline cases.

**Common misconceptions.** Judging category by appearance rather than criteria.
Believing dissolving destroys a substance. Thinking a size or weight alone
decides floating. Treating "alive" as obvious rather than criterion-based, which
is exactly why the virus case is so productive.

**Appropriate Show Your Thinking.** Explain in One Sentence, where the student
states the deciding criterion and applies it, is the crisp fit. Mini CER works
when the classification is genuinely arguable and the student must defend a call
with evidence.

**Flagship lessons.** `lesson_what-is-life.html` is the clearest and most
distinctive example in the whole curriculum. Also `lesson_cell-types.html`,
`lesson_chemical-reactions.html`, `lesson_pure-substances-and-mixtures.html`,
`lesson_physical-properties.html`, and `lesson_measuring-matter.html`. The
`game_is-it-alive.html` and `investigation_gray-zone.html` resources are pure
expressions of this thinking.

## Archetype 5: Design and Decisions

**Central thinking.** The student reasons about human choices under constraints:
how to build something well when no option is perfect, and how a technology or
human activity changes the world for better and worse. The payoff question is
"what should we build or choose, and what does it cost." Unlike the first four,
this archetype is not about explaining nature. It is about deciding and creating.

**Instructional purpose.** To teach that engineering and socio-scientific
questions rarely have a single right answer, only better and worse tradeoffs, and
that evaluating those tradeoffs is a rigorous kind of thinking, not just opinion.
This includes media and technology literacy, where the "design" being analyzed is
one aimed at the student.

**Typical lesson flow.** The lesson frames a goal and its constraints, surfaces
the competing priorities (cost against strength, speed against safety,
convenience against environmental harm, engagement against honesty), and asks the
student to weigh them and defend a choice. The struggle is that improving one
thing worsens another, and there is no way to optimize everything at once.

**Phenomena that work best.** Real design and decision problems with visible
tradeoffs: why a bicycle uses many materials, why every feature cannot be
maximized, how a rough idea becomes a mass product, how a technology reshapes a
society, and how a ragebait headline is engineered to hijack attention.

**Interactives that support it best.** Constraint and tradeoff tools: sliders
that show one gain costing another, timelines of an iterative design process,
challenges where the student must satisfy competing requirements and cannot win
on every axis at once. The engineering challenge resource type is built for this
archetype.

**Common misconceptions.** That there is one correct design. That the newest or
most expensive option is always best. That optimizing every feature at once is
possible. In the media-literacy case, that content the student sees is neutral
rather than designed to provoke a reaction.

**Appropriate Show Your Thinking.** Mini CER is the strongest fit, because a
defended choice is a claim supported by evidence and reasoning about tradeoffs.
Build the Explanation works when the student sequences the design process or
lays out the chain of consequences of a technology.

**Flagship lessons.** `lesson_engineering-design.html` and
`lesson_design-tradeoffs.html` are the core. Also
`lesson_choosing-materials.html`, `lesson_designing-to-scale.html`,
`lesson_innovation-and-sustainability.html`,
`lesson_technology-and-society.html`, and `lesson_ragebaiting.html`, which
extends the archetype from engineering into media literacy while keeping the same
thinking: a designed thing, a set of tradeoffs, and a judgment to defend.

## Merge and Keep Decisions

The brief asked for the smallest number of archetypes needed, and for explicit
reasoning wherever lessons were combined, split, or a candidate archetype was
rejected. The decisions:

**Astronomy is not a separate archetype (rejected).** The astronomy cluster
(eclipses, moon phases, sun-Earth-Moon, gravity) is the most misconception-dense
content in the program, and the HQIM audit gave it its own structural tier. It
was tempting to make it a sixth archetype. It was rejected because a tier is a
statement about how finished a lesson is, not about what kind of thinking it
asks. The thinking in these lessons is mechanism: why the Moon does not crash,
why eclipses do not happen monthly, why the phase is not a shadow. They are
mechanism lessons with unusually strong intuitions to overturn and an unusually
spatial model to build. That is a difficulty dimension inside Archetype 1, not a
different kind of science learning. Naming a separate archetype would confuse
"hard to teach" with "different to teach."

**Systems is kept separate from Mechanism (split retained).** Reasoning covered
above under Archetype 2. The short version: different phenomena, different
interactive, different misconception, so a director would design them
differently.

**Scientific method folds into Evidence and Inference (merged).**
`lesson_conducting-experiments.html` could have anchored a "science practices"
archetype of one. It was merged into Evidence and Inference because its purpose
is the same: how a trustworthy conclusion is produced from evidence. One lesson
does not justify an archetype when it fits an existing one cleanly.

**Media literacy folds into Design and Decisions (merged).**
`lesson_ragebaiting.html` looks like an outlier, but its thinking is identical to
the engineering lessons: something was designed, it carries tradeoffs, and the
student must judge it. Keeping it inside Design and Decisions keeps the archetype
count honest and reveals a real kinship a director should preserve.

**Hybrid lessons are assigned a primary archetype, not a new one.** A few lessons
genuinely span two archetypes: biological evolution (mechanism of natural
selection, plus the fossil evidence for it), Earth's place in the universe
(scale and orientation, plus inference from starlight), and gravity (orbital
mechanism, plus the astronomy misconception load). These are noted as boundary
lessons rather than used to spawn new archetypes. Real curricula have hybrids;
inventing an archetype for each blend would defeat the goal.

  conducting-experiments, biological-evolution (secondary: Mechanism),
  earths-place-in-the-universe (secondary: Mechanism).
* **Classification and Identity:** what-is-life, cell-types, chemical-reactions,
  pure-substances-and-mixtures, physical-properties, measuring-matter.
* **Design and Decisions:** engineering-design, design-tradeoffs,
  choosing-materials, designing-to-scale, innovation-and-sustainability,
  technology-and-society, ragebaiting.

The supporting resource library lines up with the same five, which is
independent confirmation the clusters are real: spatial orbit and gravity
simulations serve Mechanism, the beetle and population models serve Systems,
fossil hunts and evidence extensions serve Evidence and Inference, the is-it-alive
and gray-zone resources serve Classification, and the engineering challenge type
serves Design and Decisions.

## What This Analysis Concludes

LyfeLabz is not a flat pile of lessons on a shared template, and it is not a
sprawl of unique one-off designs either. It is five recognizable kinds of science
learning experience, each asking for a distinct kind of thinking:

1. Explain why a phenomenon happens.
2. Trace how a system behaves.
3. Infer the unseen from evidence.
4. Decide what something is by its criteria.
5. Weigh a design or a decision by its tradeoffs.

The HQIM framework already gives all five the same strong instructional
scaffolding. These archetypes are the layer beneath that scaffolding: the reason
two structurally identical lessons can still be teaching two different kinds of
science. Naming them is not a call to build templates. It is a way to see the
curriculum for what it actually is, so that future lessons can be designed with a
clear answer to the first question a curriculum director should ask: what kind of
thinking is this lesson really for.
