Day 2

Build and Test

Today you will build your first prototype, test it, and use what you learn to plan your next revision.

Today's work

You will build the first version of your ball run and run several trials. Your first design does not have to be perfect. Engineers learn the most from what does not work yet.

A first try that fails is still useful. Every trial tells you something. Your job today is to notice what happened, write it down, and use that evidence to decide what to change tomorrow.

Learning target

I can build a prototype, test it, and use evidence to plan a revision.

By the end of today you should be able to describe what happened during your trials and choose one change to make tomorrow, based on what you saw.

Build instructions

Use only the paper and tape from your challenge kit. Work with your team.

Build your first prototype

  1. Talk with your team for two minutes. Agree on the shape of your first design.
  2. Sketch your idea on scratch paper before you build. A quick drawing is enough.
  3. Build a start point that is higher than the finish point. The ball needs somewhere to roll from.
  4. Add at least two features that could slow the ball down. Turns, ramps, or paper walls all count.
  5. Make sure your structure can stand on its own. It should not fall over between trials.
  6. When you are ready to test, place the ball at the start and let go. Do not push it.

Engineering Tip

A short tip from real engineers.

Change one thing at a time. If you change three things at once and the ball goes slower, you will not know which change actually did it.

Real World

Where engineers use this kind of thinking.

Roller coaster designers, car crash safety teams, and video game physics engineers all run trial after trial and revise their designs the same way you are working today.
